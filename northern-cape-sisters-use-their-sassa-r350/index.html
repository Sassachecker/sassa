Northern Cape Sisters Use Their SASSA R350 Grant

  • Post published:March 18, 2023

One of South Africa’s least populated regions, the Northern Cape is renowned for its natural environment, scorching temperatures, and restricted resource access. Numerous region residents were left jobless or without income in March 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic and its economic impact. An R350 grant was made available by the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) to assist jobless people. Many people in the region have significantly benefited from the grant, including two sisters named Vuyelwa and Ntombizodwa Ngcengele, who grew their grant into a successful farming business. Let’s check how northern cape sisters use their Sassa r350 grant to farming business?

How Northern Cape Sisters Use Their SASSA R350 Grant to Farming Business?

Background on the Sisters

Vuyelwa and Ntombizodwa Ngcengele were born and raised in a farming family in the Northern Cape. To provide for their family, their parents ran a modest farm. The family was compelled to sell the farm due to an extended drought and financial issues, leaving Vuyelwa and Ntombizodwa without a source of income. Despite the setback, the sisters were determined to start their farming business and carry on their family’s custom.

Starting the Farming Business

It was not a simple path for the sisters to begin their farming business. They had few resources and had to overcome many obstacles, such as a lack of access to water, dry soil, and an understanding of contemporary farming methods. They were determined to make it work, though. To clear a plot of land that had been left fallow for many years, they used their SASSA R350 funding to purchase seeds, tools, and fertilizer. In addition, they were given support and guidance from a regional agricultural group, enabling them to learn cutting-edge farming practices and connect with other local farmers.

The sisters initially cultivated various crops, such as beans, tomatoes, and spinach. They put a lot of effort into tending their crops, and finally, they produced a good crop that they could sell at the neighborhood market. They could expand their activities and reinvest in their company due to their income.

Expanding the Business

The early success of Vuyelwa and Ntombizodwa assured them to grow their farming business. They used their earnings to purchase land and grow crops like maize, watermelons, and sweet potatoes. They also began keeping chickens and selling eggs, a successful business. In addition, they implemented a drip irrigation system, which uses water effectively and reduces waste, to address the problems associated with water scarcity. They also began using organic farming techniques, resulting in healthier crops and less environmental damage.

Impact on the Community

The accomplishments of Vuyelwa and Ntombizodwa have had an enormous impact on their surroundings. They have established themselves as role models for other women in the community and cooperated with other female farmers to share resources and expertise. The cooperative has given other local women a chance to benefit from their knowledge and gain access to the tools they need to begin their farming businesses.

The sisters also began a feeding program that serves nutritious food to kids in the neighborhood. The food program has been essential, particularly during the COVID-19 pandemic when many families had financial challenges. In addition, the sisters’ farming business has given them a living, improved their neighborhood, and encouraged others to follow their goals.

Current State of the Business

Today, the farming business owned by Vuyelwa and Ntombizodwa is thriving. With the help of several locals, they have extended their business to include a greenhouse and a fruit orchard. To expand the number of consumers and their company, the sisters have also begun to explore new markets, such as selling their products online.

Conclusion of Northern Cape Sisters Use Their SASSA

The success of the farming business owned by Vuyelwa and Ntombizodwa Ngcengele is a testament to the strength of perseverance, diligence, and creativity. Despite numerous obstacles, the sisters used their SASSA R350 grant to launch a successful company that gives them a living and improves the area where they live.

Their strong commitment to carrying on their family’s heritage and openness to change have made them successful. They have demonstrated that, even in the most challenging settings, anyone can launch a prosperous farming business with adequate resources, assistance, and training.

The significance of small-scale farmers in South Africa’s food system is also brought home by their stories. Small-scale farms produce fresh, wholesome, and sustainable food while creating jobs and boosting the local economy. Supporting and empowering small-scale farmers like Vuyelwa and Ntombizodwa is essential as the nation strives to establish a more sustainable and resilient food system.

In conclusion, the farming business run by the Ngcengele sisters is an outstanding example of how the SASSA R350 grant can encourage entrepreneurship and improve local communities. Their stories motivate other jobless people struggling to make ends meet and searching for opportunities to start their businesses. Investing in small-scale farming can produce a more resilient, sustainable, and inclusive food system that helps all.
